# Discussion Failure Modes

## DISC-F1: Results Summary Without Interpretation

- What it looks like: the Discussion restates result paragraphs but does not explain meaning, limits, or relation to aims.
- Why it hurts: misses the section's evaluative function.
- How to detect it: few sentences contain interpretive, comparative, or limitation signals.
- Suggested revision action: reorganize around aim answer, interpretation, comparison, limitation, and implication.

## DISC-F2: Overstated Translation

- What it looks like: clinical or therapeutic language exceeds preclinical, correlative, or computational evidence.
- Why it hurts: weakens scientific credibility and may be marked as overclaiming.
- How to detect it: implication claims use clinical certainty without clinical evidence.
- Suggested revision action: downgrade to appropriately cautious language and state the evidence level.

## DISC-F3: Generic Limitations

- What it looks like: limitations are broad phrases that do not affect any specific claim.
- Why it hurts: looks formulaic and does not help readers calibrate findings.
- How to detect it: the limitation cannot be paired with a result or interpretation.
- Suggested revision action: name the affected claim and explain the likely direction or type of uncertainty.

## DISC-F4: Missing Contradictory Evidence

- What it looks like: only supportive literature is discussed despite known uncertainty or conflicting findings.
- Why it hurts: makes the argument appear biased.
- How to detect it: major claims have citations only to agreement or reviews, with no contrast or caveat.
- Suggested revision action: add a short contrast paragraph and explain why differences may exist.

## DISC-F5: New Unsourced Claims

- What it looks like: major biological, clinical, or methodological claims appear for the first time in Discussion without citation or result support.
- Why it hurts: breaks evidence chain and can create factual risk.
- How to detect it: claim cannot be traced to Results or source evidence index.
- Suggested revision action: cite, move to Introduction/background, support with Results, or remove.

## DISC-F6: Translational Leap

- What it looks like: the Discussion jumps from in vitro, animal, biospecimen, computational, or portal-derived evidence to patient treatment implications.
- Why it hurts: overstates evidence and can mislead the reader about clinical readiness.
- How to detect it: clinical verbs or treatment recommendations lack clinical trial, validation, or direct patient-outcome evidence.
- Suggested revision action: restate as hypothesis-generating, mechanistic, or requiring validation.

## DISC-F7: Prediction Model Readiness Overclaim

- What it looks like: a model is presented as useful in practice based only on development-set or internal-validation performance.
- Why it hurts: ignores generalizability, calibration, external validation, and clinical utility.
- How to detect it: no discussion of external validation, calibration, intended-use population, or deployment context.
- Suggested revision action: add validation status, performance limits, and intended-use caveats.

## DISC-F8: Real-World Data Causal Inflation

- What it looks like: registry, EHR, public portal, or clinico-genomic associations are described as causal mechanism, treatment effect, or clinical actionability.
- Why it hurts: observational and public-data designs often carry selection, missingness, linkage, and endpoint-definition limitations.
- How to detect it: clinical or causal language appears without trial, functional, external-validation, or causal-inference support.
- Suggested revision action: recalibrate as association or hypothesis generation and add design-specific limitations.

## DISC-F9: Pan-Cancer Overgeneralization

- What it looks like: a finding from a pan-cancer or cross-tumor analysis is generalized to all cancers or a specific clinical implication without tumor-type, platform, cohort, or validation caveats.
- Why it hurts: cross-cancer patterns can hide lineage-specific biology and data-generation differences.
- How to detect it: broad cancer-wide wording appears without naming included tumor types, data tiers, or validation evidence.
- Suggested revision action: specify the supported cancer contexts and downgrade unsupported generalization.

## DISC-F10: Enrichment Treated As Mechanism

- What it looks like: enriched pathways, gene sets, marker clusters, or spatial neighborhoods are discussed as direct mechanistic proof.
- Why it hurts: enrichment depends on database annotations, background sets, thresholds, and correlation structure.
- How to detect it: the draft moves from an enrichment result to causal or therapeutic language without direct validation.
- Suggested revision action: state enrichment as supportive/hypothesis-generating and add validation needs.
