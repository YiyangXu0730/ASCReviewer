# Good Introduction Patterns

## INT-P1: Context -> Gap -> Aim

- Description: Establish only the context needed to understand the problem, identify the specific knowledge or practice gap, and end with an explicit aim, hypothesis, question, or review objective.
- When it applies: introductions for dissertations, thesis chapters, biomedical papers, proposals, and review papers.
- Source/provenance ID: `INTRO-R1-CONTEXT-GAP-AIM`; `PATTERN-PAPER-STRUCTURE-2017`; `GUIDE-STROBE-2007`.
- Positive example summary: A biomedical introduction starts with disease or field context, narrows to the unresolved mechanism or evidence gap, then states what the chapter/article will evaluate.
- Failure mode: broad literature dump with no reviewable gap.
- Review checklist use: Ask whether a marker could point to one sentence that states the gap and one sentence that states the aim.

## INT-P2: Rubric-Matched Scope

- Description: The introduction previews exactly the scope required by the task, neither omitting required concepts nor expanding into unsupported side topics.
- When it applies: any assessed writing where a section brief or marking scheme defines scope.
- Source/provenance ID: `RUBRIC-R1-RUBRIC-FIRST`.
- Positive example summary: A dissertation introduction includes required theory, clinical relevance, and rationale because the marking scheme requests them, but defers methods details to the Methods section.
- Failure mode: impressive but irrelevant background displaces high-weight rubric material.
- Review checklist use: Map each paragraph to a rubric criterion or justified context role.

## INT-P3: Claim Calibration at the Rationale Stage

- Description: Background and rationale claims should reflect evidence strength and avoid promising outcomes the draft cannot support.
- When it applies: biomedical, translational, cancer biology, genomics, and mechanism-heavy writing.
- Source/provenance ID: `INTRO-R2-NO-OVERCLAIM`; `CLAIMS-R1-EVIDENCE-CALIBRATION`.
- Positive example summary: The Introduction describes an association or plausible vulnerability as a rationale, not as an established therapy or confirmed mechanism unless evidence supports that level of certainty.
- Failure mode: certainty exceeds the evidence type, such as treating exploratory or indirect evidence as proven clinical effect.
- Review checklist use: Underline verbs of certainty and check whether citations and study design justify them.

## INT-P4: Signpost Without Spoiling the Results

- Description: The Introduction can state the direction and purpose of the work but should not report detailed findings before the Results.
- When it applies: empirical manuscripts and dissertation results chapters.
- Source/provenance ID: `PATTERN-PAPER-STRUCTURE-2017`; `GUIDE-ICMJE-MANUSCRIPT`.
- Positive example summary: The final paragraph states what the study/chapter investigates and why, leaving numerical outcomes for Results.
- Failure mode: Introduction includes extensive result values, effect sizes, or interpretive conclusions.
- Review checklist use: Flag any sentence that sounds like a result and ask whether it belongs later.

## INT-P5: Study-Design-Aware Rationale

- Description: The rationale should fit the study design: systematic reviews justify search scope, observational studies justify exposures/outcomes, biomarker studies justify marker choice, and computational studies justify data and pipeline choices.
- When it applies: when the task declares a specific research design.
- Source/provenance ID: `METHODS-R2-STUDY-DESIGN-GUIDELINE`; `GUIDE-EQUATOR-LIBRARY`.
- Positive example summary: A biomarker dissertation introduction explains why the marker and outcome matter before Methods explain specimens and assays.
- Failure mode: disease-topic background ignores the actual design the reader must evaluate.
- Review checklist use: Identify the study type and check whether the Introduction prepares the reader for that design.

## INT-P6: Evidence-Level Rationale

- Description: The Introduction should distinguish exploratory, mechanistic, preclinical, biospecimen, public-data, prediction-model, and clinical evidence when those distinctions affect the rationale.
- When it applies: biomedical, cancer biology, translational, public genomics, biomarker, and prediction-model writing.
- Source/provenance ID: `CLAIMS-R2-TRANSLATIONAL-EVIDENCE-LADDER`; `INTRO-R4-PUBLIC-DATA-RATIONALE-LIMITS`.
- Positive example summary: A public cancer-genomics association is introduced as a hypothesis-generating rationale unless independently validated or supported by direct experimental evidence.
- Failure mode: portal-derived or preclinical evidence is introduced as if it already proves clinical actionability.
- Review checklist use: Label the evidence level behind the rationale before evaluating claim strength.
- When it does not apply: purely theoretical, humanities, or non-evidence-based reflective writing unless the rubric asks for evidence grading.

## INT-P7: Guideline Scope Without Guideline Dumping

- Description: Introductions can signal that a study is a trial, observational study, systematic review, animal experiment, biospecimen study, or prediction-model study, but should not reproduce a reporting checklist in the Introduction.
- When it applies: tasks with a clearly identified study design.
- Source/provenance ID: `INTRO-R3-STUDY-TYPE-SCOPE-SIGNAL`; `METHODS-R2-STUDY-DESIGN-GUIDELINE`.
- Positive example summary: A prediction-model introduction explains the clinical problem and why prediction is needed, while Methods handle TRIPOD-specific reporting details.
- Failure mode: Introduction either hides the study type or becomes a checklist-style Methods preview.
- Review checklist use: Check whether study type is discoverable and justified without displacing gap/aim logic.
- When it does not apply: assignments that ask for broad background only or where study design is not yet declared.
