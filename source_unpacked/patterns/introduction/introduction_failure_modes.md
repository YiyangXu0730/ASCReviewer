# Introduction Failure Modes

## INT-F1: Literature Dump Without Gap

- What it looks like: paragraphs summarize many papers, mechanisms, or definitions but never state what remains unknown.
- Why it hurts: weakens rationale and makes aim/rubric alignment hard to assess.
- How to detect it: no sentence contains a clear gap signal such as limited evidence, unclear mechanism, unresolved question, or missing synthesis.
- Suggested revision action: compress background and add a gap sentence immediately before the aim.

## INT-F2: Aim Missing or Hidden

- What it looks like: the reader must infer the purpose from topic coverage.
- Why it hurts: markers cannot judge whether later sections answer the intended question.
- How to detect it: no explicit aim, objective, hypothesis, research question, or review purpose appears near the end.
- Suggested revision action: add a concise aim statement tied to rubric criteria and section scope.

## INT-F3: Overclaiming Biomedical Significance

- What it looks like: exploratory, preclinical, correlative, or indirect evidence is framed as established mechanism or clinical benefit.
- Why it hurts: damages scientific credibility and may mislead the Discussion.
- How to detect it: certainty verbs such as proves, confirms, establishes, or will treat lack direct supporting evidence.
- Suggested revision action: downgrade language and cite evidence type accurately.

## INT-F4: Rubric-Irrelevant Background

- What it looks like: interesting domain context dominates while required section elements are absent.
- Why it hurts: uses word count on material that may not earn marks.
- How to detect it: paragraphs cannot be mapped to a marking criterion, required concept, or necessary transition.
- Suggested revision action: cut or move low-value background and insert missing rubric elements.

## INT-F5: Topic Hardcoding in Generic Review

- What it looks like: a reusable review checklist assumes one disease, pathway, or dissertation topic.
- Why it hurts: makes ASCReview non-reusable and violates specialization rules.
- How to detect it: generic skill text names a seed topic as if it always applies.
- Suggested revision action: move topic details into task specialization and keep core wording design- or section-based.

## INT-F6: Guideline Laundering

- What it looks like: a reporting guideline is used to create Introduction criticism even though the task design has not been established.
- Why it hurts: converts official guidance into false scoring authority and can distract from the teacher rubric.
- How to detect it: the review says a section "must follow" PRISMA, ARRIVE, TRIPOD, or another guideline without a specialization decision.
- Suggested revision action: mark the guideline as potentially relevant and defer application until study design is confirmed.

## INT-F7: Public-Data Overclaim

- What it looks like: public portal access, mutation frequency, or an exploratory association is framed as direct mechanistic or clinical proof.
- Why it hurts: weakens scientific accuracy before the Methods and Results can establish evidence strength.
- How to detect it: rationale claims jump from database observation to mechanism, prognosis, or therapy without validation language.
- Suggested revision action: classify the evidence level and revise claim strength to match the actual source.
